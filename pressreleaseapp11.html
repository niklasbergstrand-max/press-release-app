This can be meaningful surveillance results, epidemiological trends, risk assessment findings, etc. A press release should use statistics strategically: enough to support the message, but not so much that they overwhelm the reader. One to three well-chosen, impactful stats are usually sufficient to establish credibility, highlight urgency, or illustrate trends. Avoid cluttering the release with excessive figures or technical data. Journalists and the public appreciate large round numbers or comparisons given for a particular time duration and region. Frame figures in terms of their impact on people (deaths, hospitalisations, ‘1 in X’ people) or the impact on systems (healthcare pressure, staff shortages) to humanise messages.
Numbers showing rising or falling trends may be newsworthy and attract media attention. Data that show shortfalls tend to drive strong media uptake, especially when linked to responsibility or urgency. How does it compare with historical data?
Internal procedures, meetings, decisions-in-progress, or bureaucratic developments fail to capture the attention of the media and the wider public as they are not newsworthy. Readers wants to know what affects them, not how a decision was made. A press release about a committee meeting or a draft framework rarely answers the question: ‘Why should I care?’.
